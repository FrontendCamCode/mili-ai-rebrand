After working with hundreds of asset-intensive organisations across mining, utilities, oil and gas, and defence, we've seen MRO operations across the full spectrum. Some are genuinely excellent — lean, reliable, data-driven. Most are somewhere in the middle. A meaningful number are in real trouble and don't fully know it yet.

Here's an honest assessment of where the industry stands.

## The Good

**Technology adoption is accelerating.** Five years ago, it was unusual to find a maintenance organisation actively investing in purpose-built MRO technology beyond their ERP. Today it's common. Operations managers understand the gap between what their ERP can do and what a specialist platform provides. The appetite for better tools is real.

**Senior leadership attention has increased.** Supply chain disruptions during 2020–2022 brought indirect material management into board conversations for the first time in many organisations. CFOs who previously ignored MRO inventory are now asking pointed questions about working capital tied up in spare parts. The attention is welcome, even if it's created pressure without always being accompanied by adequate investment.

**Data availability has improved dramatically.** Most large ERP systems now produce transaction data at a level of granularity that would have required significant custom development a decade ago. The raw material for better MRO decisions — purchasing history, consumption patterns, inventory movements — exists. The question is whether it's being used.

## The Bad

**The talent pipeline is thin.** Experienced MRO practitioners — people who understand the intersection of maintenance strategy, inventory management, and supply chain operations — are scarce and getting scarcer. The discipline hasn't attracted the same graduate cohort that direct procurement has. Operations are running on institutional knowledge that isn't being systematically captured or transferred.

**Material master quality remains the persistent problem.** Despite years of investment in ERP and CMMS systems, most organisations still have significant proportions of their material records in poor condition — missing specifications, inconsistent naming, unresolved duplicates. The problem is well-understood. The will to address it systematically is less consistent.

**Performance measurement is weak.** Most operations measure what's easy to measure — stockout rates, inventory value, purchase order cycle time — without connecting these metrics to operational outcomes. High inventory value and frequent stockouts can coexist. The metrics that would explain why are often absent: demand accuracy, stocking parameter validity, supplier reliability by lead time, free text spend as a proportion of total.

## The Ugly

**The free text problem is worse than most people admit.** When we first scope a client's MRO environment, we ask what percentage of purchase orders use free text rather than material numbers. The answer is almost always "very few." When we actually measure it, it's almost always north of 20%, frequently 30–40%. This spend is uncontrolled, unanalysed, and the source of a disproportionate amount of compliance risk and duplicate inventory.

**Emergency purchasing has become the norm.** In a well-functioning MRO supply chain, emergency freight is an exception — a genuine operational surprise, not a routine workaround. In many operations, emergency purchasing has been normalised to the point where it's budgeted. Teams treat it as a cost of doing business rather than a signal of systematic failure in planning, stocking, and supplier management.

**Digital transformation is creating a false sense of progress.** ERP upgrades, new mobile maintenance platforms, IoT sensor deployments — these projects generate executive attention and project budgets. They rarely fix the underlying data quality and process discipline problems that drive poor MRO outcomes. The result is modern infrastructure running on bad data, producing bad decisions faster and more efficiently than the old systems did.

---

The industry is moving in the right direction. The pace is slow, the problems are persistent, and the progress is uneven. But the organisations that combine senior leadership commitment, data quality investment, and operational discipline are achieving measurable results — less inventory, fewer stockouts, lower emergency purchasing spend. The model works. The execution challenge is sustaining it.
