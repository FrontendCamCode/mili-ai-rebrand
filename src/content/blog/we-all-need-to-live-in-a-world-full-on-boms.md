Ask a maintenance planner what they need most, and you'll hear the same answer across industries: "I need to know what parts are on what equipment."

It's a deceptively simple request. In most operations, it's deceptively hard to fulfil.

## What a BOM actually is in a maintenance context

In manufacturing, a bill of material defines what goes into making a product. In asset maintenance, a BOM defines what materials are associated with maintaining an asset — the consumables, spare parts, and replacement components that a piece of equipment might require during its operational life.

A complete BOM tells a planner, before they start scoping a job, exactly which materials to check availability for, which long-lead items to pre-order, and which spares to stage at the work site. Without it, material planning is reactive, jobs are delayed by parts chases, and emergency purchasing fills the gap at premium cost.

## The BOM debt most operations carry

The uncomfortable reality is that most large asset bases have incomplete BOMs — not because the information doesn't exist, but because nobody has put in the work to connect it to the EAM.

New equipment arrives with OEM documentation that includes recommended spare parts lists. That documentation is filed, stored, or uploaded somewhere. The material records required to represent those parts are created in the ERP or CMMS — but the link between the material record and the asset functional location is never established.

The equipment runs. Work orders are raised against it. Planners work from memory and historical work orders, not structured BOMs. The institutional knowledge lives in people's heads, not in the system.

Then someone leaves. Or a shutdown scope expands beyond what the usual team has seen. Or a piece of equipment fails in an unexpected way. And the absence of structured BOMs becomes a crisis.

## Building BOMs systematically

The path to a world of complete BOMs isn't a big-bang data migration. It's a systematic build programme that works through the asset base in criticality order.

For each piece of critical equipment, the process is:

1. Pull the OEM documentation and identify the recommended spare parts list.
2. Match each item on the list to an existing material record, or create one if it doesn't exist.
3. Link the material record to the functional location or equipment record in the EAM.
4. Validate the BOM against the equipment as-maintained, noting any modifications or substitutions.

The BOM isn't a one-time exercise. As equipment is modified, as OEM recommendations are updated, and as operational experience generates better knowledge of what's actually required, the BOMs need to reflect that. BOM maintenance should be a standing part of the maintenance planning process — not a project that ends.

## What complete BOMs enable

The downstream benefits of a world with complete BOMs are significant. Shutdown planning becomes deterministic rather than guesswork. Material availability checks can be run against a planned work scope automatically. Long-lead items can be identified and ordered with sufficient lead time. Kitting — the pre-staging of all required materials before a job starts — becomes the standard, not the exception.

Less tangibly but equally importantly: the institutional knowledge that currently lives in the heads of experienced planners gets captured in the system. The organisation becomes less dependent on individuals and more resilient to turnover.

We all need to live in a world full of BOMs. The question is who's going to build them.
