This is the first of two articles examining where indirect material supply chain management stands today, and where it's heading. Part one covers the current state. Part two covers where the discipline needs to go.

## Taking stock of where we are

The indirect material supply chain is one of the last significant operational domains to undergo systematic technology-enabled transformation. Direct procurement transformed in the 1990s and 2000s with e-procurement platforms, supplier portals, and sophisticated sourcing analytics. Maintenance management transformed with enterprise asset management systems. MRO — sitting at the intersection of both — has largely been managed with what was left over.

That's changing. But understanding where we are, honestly, is the precondition for charting a useful path forward.

### Material data quality: improving but insufficient

Survey after survey, conversation after conversation, the answer is the same: material master quality is the single biggest operational challenge in MRO. Too many records. Too many duplicates. Too many missing specifications. Too many descriptions that don't describe.

The improvement programmes are real. Enrichment projects run. Records are cleaned. And then, without robust governance, the quality degrades again. The problem is not the cleaning — it's the maintenance. Most operations can improve their material data. Few have built the process infrastructure to keep it improved.

### Stocking optimisation: static parameters in a dynamic world

The theory of MRO inventory optimisation is well-established. Safety stock calculations, EOQ models, criticality-weighted investment decisions — the academic and practitioner literature covers the ground thoroughly. The practice is messier.

Most operations set stocking parameters at implementation and revisit them sporadically, if at all. Consumption patterns change. Lead times shift. Assets are retired and new ones commissioned. The parameters don't keep up. The result is stocking strategies that were designed for an operation that no longer exists.

Systematic stocking optimisation — regular, data-driven review of reorder points and quantities across the full stocked item base — is rare. It's not technically difficult. It requires process discipline and reliable input data, both of which are harder to sustain than the calculation itself.

### Technology: capability ahead of adoption

The technology available for MRO management has advanced significantly. Purpose-built MRO platforms, sophisticated analytics tools, mobile storeroom applications, integration middleware — the toolset available to an operations team today is far richer than it was five years ago.

The adoption challenge is the gap between what the technology can do and what the organisation can actually absorb. Implementations that move too fast, ahead of data quality and process maturity, create expensive disappointments. The organisations getting the most from MRO technology are those that have invested in the fundamentals — data quality, process design, people capability — before or alongside the technology investment.

### The talent challenge: knowledge without succession

The deep practical knowledge required to manage an MRO supply chain well — understanding of maintenance strategy, supply chain economics, supplier relationships, system configuration — takes years to develop. Much of it is held by people who are approaching the end of their careers.

The succession challenge is acute. Graduate programmes don't produce MRO specialists. The discipline doesn't have the profile to attract the analytical talent that went into direct procurement. Operations are running on institutional knowledge that isn't being systematically transferred to the next generation.
