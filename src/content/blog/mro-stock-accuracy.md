Stock accuracy — the degree to which system inventory records match physical reality — is one of the most foundational metrics in MRO operations. It is also one of the most commonly neglected.

The assumption is that if something goes wrong, someone will notice. A technician can't find a part. A purchase order is raised. The part turns up. In a world where most individual errors are self-correcting, the cost of bad accuracy isn't always visible.

But the cumulative cost of operating below threshold accuracy is substantial, and the self-correction mechanism has a price.

## What below-threshold accuracy actually costs

**Phantom stock.** When the system shows inventory that doesn't physically exist, planners and buyers trust the record. They don't order replenishment. The stockout arrives unannounced. The emergency response is costly.

**Ghost demand.** When physical stock exists that the system doesn't know about, duplicate orders are raised. Inventory grows. Working capital is consumed providing stock that was already there.

**Planning noise.** MRP and automated reorder systems use system stock quantities as inputs. When those quantities are wrong, the outputs — suggested orders, planned job material lists, shortage alerts — are also wrong. Planners learn not to trust the system. They revert to manual checks and workarounds.

**Audit and compliance exposure.** Inventory is a balance sheet asset. Material inaccuracy means the balance sheet is wrong. In regulated industries, this creates compliance risk that extends beyond operational inconvenience.

The threshold below which these costs become systemic is typically cited as 90–95% location accuracy. Below this, the volume of errors is high enough to degrade planning decisions across the board. Most operations, if they measure it honestly, are below this threshold.

## How accuracy degrades

Accuracy doesn't collapse overnight. It degrades through accumulated small failures:

- Stock is issued without system update ("I'll do the transaction later").
- Goods are received into the wrong location.
- Items are used from a bin and the quantity isn't adjusted.
- Physical moves aren't reflected in system records.
- Cycle counts are done infrequently and results aren't acted on.

Each individual failure is minor. Over months and years, they compound into a significant gap between the system picture and physical reality.

## The correction approach

Recovering stock accuracy is disciplined work. The starting point is understanding the current state — not through a full annual stocktake, but through a targeted cycle count programme that focuses on high-value, high-movement, or known-problem materials first.

For each discrepancy found during cycle counting, the discipline is to investigate root cause before correcting the record. Simply adjusting the system quantity without understanding why the error occurred ensures it will recur.

The process changes required to prevent degradation include:

- Real-time or near-real-time transaction processing. Issues, receipts, and moves recorded at the point of occurrence, not batched at end of shift.
- Location discipline. Every item has a designated home. Unlocated stock is treated as a system failure, not a normal condition.
- Regular cycle counting as a standing activity, not a project. The frequency of counting for a given material should be proportional to its value and the consequence of an error.

Recovering accuracy is a 6–18 month effort for a large, mature storeroom with deep-seated problems. Maintaining accuracy is an ongoing operational discipline. Both require commitment from operations, stores, and procurement leadership — not just the storeroom team.

The payoff is a system that planners and buyers can actually trust. And a planning system you can trust is worth considerably more than one you can't.
