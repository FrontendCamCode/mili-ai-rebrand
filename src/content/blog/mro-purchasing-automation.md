Every procurement leader in an asset-intensive organisation knows the answer to this question before they're asked: how much of your purchasing activity is still done manually?

Too much. They know it. Their team knows it. The ERP system has had automated reordering capability for years. And yet, every morning, someone is reviewing a list of materials, deciding what to order, and raising purchase orders one by one.

Why does manual purchasing persist? And where does automation actually help?

## The honest reasons manual work survives

**Institutional distrust of system parameters.** Reorder points and reorder quantities were set years ago. Nobody has maintained them. Buyers have learned through experience that the system will suggest orders for things that don't need ordering, and miss things that do. So they override it. Constantly. The manual work is a rational response to bad data.

**Complexity at the exception layer.** Automated reordering handles routine replenishment reasonably well. It handles exceptions — supplier OOS notifications, emergency needs, specification changes, expedited orders — not at all. The buyer's job increasingly becomes exception management, which resists automation by definition.

**Comfort and control.** This one rarely makes it into the official analysis. Experienced buyers have developed considerable expertise in navigating their organisation's supply chain. Automation threatens to make that expertise invisible. The preference for manual work is sometimes about professional identity, not just process logic.

**Approval workflow friction.** In many organisations, automated purchase orders still require manual approval. If the approval process takes as long as the order itself, the efficiency gain from automation disappears in the back half of the process.

## Where automation genuinely helps

Despite these barriers, there are areas where purchasing automation delivers clear, sustainable value:

**Routine replenishment of well-defined items.** Materials with stable demand, reliable suppliers, and accurate system parameters are the ideal automation candidates. Blanket orders with call-off schedules, vendor-managed inventory, and automated reorder triggers with minimal human intervention.

**Demand signal processing.** Converting planned maintenance work into material requirements, checking availability, and flagging gaps — this is mechanical work that buyers shouldn't be doing manually. A system that translates a planned job from the CMMS into a material availability check and a purchase requisition saves meaningful time.

**Supplier performance monitoring.** Tracking actual versus quoted lead times, flagging under-performing suppliers, and triggering review conversations is amenable to automation. Most organisations do this with a spreadsheet maintained by someone who does it when they have time.

**Spend analysis and category reporting.** Automated aggregation of MRO spend by category, supplier, and material group — refreshed weekly rather than quarterly — gives procurement leaders the visibility to negotiate better terms and identify consolidation opportunities.

## The precondition nobody talks about

All of this depends on one thing: material master data quality. Automated reordering with bad system parameters makes bad decisions at scale. Automated demand processing with duplicate material records generates duplicate purchase orders. Spend analytics with uncatalogued free text purchases show you only the part of the spend you can see.

Automation is a multiplier. It multiplies the quality of your underlying data — good or bad. The organisations that try to automate their way out of a data quality problem find that the volume of work doesn't decrease. It just gets done faster, and wrongly.

The path to genuine purchasing automation runs through the material master. Fix the data first. Then let the system run.
