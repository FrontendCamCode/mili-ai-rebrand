Part one of this series examined where indirect material supply chain management stands today — the persistent data quality challenges, the stocking optimisation gap, and the talent succession problem. This article turns to where the discipline needs to go.

## The near-term trajectory

### AI will change what's possible, not what's required

The arrival of capable large language models and agentic AI systems has generated significant excitement in the MRO space. The potential is real: material description generation, specification extraction from OEM documentation, automated duplicate identification, intelligent demand sensing. Genuinely useful capabilities are emerging.

But AI amplifies the quality of underlying data — it doesn't replace the need for it. A language model that generates material descriptions from unstructured text is impressive. A language model that generates material descriptions from unstructured text and writes them into a material master full of existing duplicates and incorrect specifications creates sophisticated-looking problems faster than manual processes would.

The sequence matters. Data quality first. Automation and AI second. The organisations that try to use AI to shortcut the data quality work will find that the shortcut doesn't exist.

What AI genuinely changes is the economics of data quality work itself. Tasks that required significant human effort — cleaning thousands of material descriptions, extracting specifications from PDFs, cross-referencing manufacturer part numbers — can be done at a fraction of the previous cost and time. This makes the data quality investment more accessible and more defensible. The work still needs to be done. AI makes it cheaper to do.

### The case for operational specialisation

MRO has historically been managed as a sub-function of broader maintenance or procurement organisations. This made sense when the complexity was manageable. It makes less sense as supply chains become more global, more exposed, and more consequential to operational outcomes.

The organisations that manage MRO best are increasingly those that have built or acquired specialised capability — people who understand both the technical requirements of maintenance materials and the supply chain mechanisms that make those materials available reliably and economically.

This specialisation is emerging in large mining and utility organisations through dedicated MRO centres of excellence. It's emerging in the market through specialist consulting firms and technology providers. The direction is clear: the complexity of the discipline requires specialisation, and generalist approaches are increasingly inadequate.

### Supply chain resilience as a strategic priority

The disruptions of 2020–2022 made supply chain resilience a board-level concern in ways that routine operational performance rarely does. The conversation has shifted from "how do we reduce inventory?" to "how do we ensure we always have what we need?"

This shift creates both opportunity and risk for MRO practitioners. The opportunity is increased investment and senior attention. The risk is that resilience investment — higher safety stock, increased domestic sourcing, dual-supplier strategies — is pursued without the analytical foundation to determine where it's actually required.

Resilience is not uniform. Some materials genuinely require higher inventory investment because of supply chain concentration risk or critical application. Others are resilient by nature — multiple suppliers, short lead times, standard specifications. Undifferentiated resilience investment is expensive and misallocated. Targeted resilience investment, built on solid criticality and lead time data, is where the value lies.

## Where the discipline needs to go

The direction is clear even if the path is uneven:

**Data quality as infrastructure, not a project.** The organisations that will manage MRO well over the next decade are those that treat material master quality as operational infrastructure — something maintained continuously through robust governance, not improved periodically through cleanup projects.

**Dynamic optimisation as standard practice.** Stocking parameters reviewed annually as a matter of course. Lead times measured from actual history. Safety stock calculated from current consumption patterns. Not every quarter for every item, but systematic enough that the parameters reflect reality.

**Technology that extends human capability.** Purpose-built platforms that do the analytical heavy lifting — duplicate identification, reorder point calculation, exception monitoring — so that experienced practitioners can focus on the decisions that require judgment rather than the mechanical work that doesn't.

**Capability built for succession.** The knowledge that currently lives in experienced practitioners needs to be captured in processes, systems, and structured training. The organisations that figure out how to transfer deep MRO expertise will be more resilient than those that rely on institutional memory that walks out the door at retirement.

The direction is good. The pace of progress needs to accelerate.
